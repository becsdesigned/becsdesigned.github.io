A study in reduction. Loupe asks: what is the minimum a brand needs to exist? The answer, it turns out, is almost nothing — a shape, a colour, an intention. Everything else is noise.

The circle as the mark. Not a logo in the traditional sense — no wordmark, no tagline — just a single form that creates presence wherever it lands. Small on a business card. Enormous on a wall. It holds either way.

Primary application

The brief

The client came with a word: loupe. A magnifying glass. A jeweller's tool. Something that brings the overlooked into focus. They wanted a brand that felt like that — precise, unassuming, quietly attentive.

The instinct was to over-design it. To make the concept literal. To draw a lens, an eye, a circle with a handle. All of that got stripped away until what remained was the thing itself: a circle. Unadorned. Confident in its plainness.

What reduction teaches

Every project that strips back to almost nothing teaches the same lesson: the temptation to add is almost always about the designer's discomfort, not the work's need. Loupe was a practice in sitting with that discomfort until it passed.

What remained after all the stripping was something that could hold attention without demanding it. That is, maybe, the goal every time.
